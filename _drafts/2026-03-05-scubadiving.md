A bucket list item achieved.

With our time in the C-4 countries (Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, and Nicaragua) quickly coming to an end as we approached our 90-day visa, we weren't sure if taking a 2 week detour into Honduras for scuba diving certification was worth it or not, as that would significantly cut our time in Nicaragua in half. In the end - with some convincing from our traveling friends - we took the plunge (pun intended) and our initial plan of only 4 days of scuba diving quickly turned into a full week. 

If you were to look at a map\comment{I think you should link our map!}, you would see that we started on the Pacific Coast of Honduras, drove all the way up to the Caribbean coast, and took a one hour ferry just to get to the island of Utila, an island known for being backpacker-friendly with very affordable certification options. This island is so tiny that bringing a campervan over would not have been feasible as the roads are not big enough to drive on. It's so tiny that the one time that a campervan did appear on the island, the mayor and the chief of police got into it, and the mayor invited the campervan to stay on his property (such is life on a small island that this story is now local legend). So for this one week, the three of us stayed at a backpacker hostel that provided accomodation with their certification. 

Our first day there, I was horrified and disheartened and absolutely did not think we were going to make it through the week. Here's why: (1) the hostel kitchen was absolutely disgusting, like flies in the fridge disgusting, (2) we were in a shared dorm with Arya and sleeping on the top bunks which wasn't ideal for her, (3) Arya was barking at newcomers at dusk time. I realize how old we sound saying that being in a shared dorm room was not great, but to be fair, we have been living in our own space this entire time and we had been told we were going to have a private room because of the dog, so expectations had been set. Luckily, the next day we got moved into a very nice private room (bonus: for free) that had AC (which we actually didn't use) and a private bathroom. This change improved things so much. For one, we could get comfortable and spread out, and Arya had a space that was hers and helped her to calm down so much. The kitchen never improved until 5 days later, but we managed it the best we could as eating out was not appealing as a vegetarian and trying to remain on budget. 

We worked with Arya on our first full day there as we did our course readings and quizzes. We practiced leaving her in the room and her not go barking mad. We practiced being calm when other people entered the hostel. She very quickly got the hang of it all. 

We even benefitted from having made friends the previous night with N (from Texas!) who had shared the dorm with us. She was very happy to have a private room all to herself when we moved out as no one moved in until her very last night. You're welcome! This is important because the next day, when we were going through safety and gear with the instructor, but Arya was in her room, which doesn't shut unless locked and we had left it unlocked...well, the wind blew open the door, and next thing you know, Arya comes trotting out all casually looking for us. N very quickly sees this and puts Arya up again before she spots us \comment{Love the sentence and idea, I get that it’s stream of conscience, but it needs to be cleaned up a little to make it more clear}. This was not the only time this happened, and between N and our vanlife friends \comment{add link to antigua post} who had shown up a day later, Arya was well taken care of and watched. She was so well-loved that one of our friends had been walking her every time we were out on the boat scuba diving (we only found this out on the last day!). We had also been walking her in our free time, but how incredibly lucky was Arya (and us) for having a friend who just did that. 

So Arya was enjoying the hostel life, and we were too.

Scuba diving itself is a whole nother story. On our first day, we practiced safety and gear use right off the beach. And the first time we went under with all of our gear on, 2m under surface, I was struggling. I believed that scuba diving was not for me and that maybe I would just learn how to free dive (which I still will). I just could not stay on the floor of the ocean and I couldn't figure out this breathing thing with gear and I kept coming up. I soon got the hang of it, and the rest of the training session went smoothly.  Clark struggled when asked to practice not having air, which is something we practice multiple times throughout the whole certification. Pretending to be out of air, asking someone to share air with you, getting the spare respirator, and purging it of water before breathing in the air was definitely a bit unnatural. Clark also got the hang of this with practice. 

Now, we were ready to go to 18m! Boy was that something. We did 4 dives over the course of two days where we practiced swimming, running into issues like googles filling up with water or not having air, descending and ascending, and trying to maintain buoyancy so we are neither sinking nor floating up. It was quite a challenge when we had to navigate between the coral reef walls without touching the coral reef with our gear. I was very excited to practice my skills in this course. 

After having completed the Open Water course, and knowing how affordable we were doing this in Utila, I convinced C to stay here for a few more days and knock out the Advanced Open Water course. This course allowed us to further define our navigation skills, go down to 30m, perfect bouyancy, explore a shipwreck, and go night diving, which is a whole other world. To achieve this and still make it to Nicaragua with enough time, we ended up doing 4 dives one day and three the next. This was insane and absolutely exhausting, but whoa. The artificial \comment{not sure this is the right word} shipwreck we visited was also a time to perfect swimming with gear on and not running into the wreck itself. We even got to see a huge snapper that had been hiding in the masthead. Now, the night dive - which was our last dive - was incredible. It's dark, so we use flashlights to observe the wildlife and our group spotted an octopus, an eel, a puffer fish, massive lobsters, a lionfish, and a humongous hermit crab (that I found!). I can see why night dives are such a you either 'love it' or 'hate it' kind of dive. 

This week has us eager for more dives, and a certain Don Hickman is already thinking about future dives we could do together.
