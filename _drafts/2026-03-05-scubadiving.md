A bucket list item achieved.

With our time in the C-4 countries (Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, and Nicaragua) quickly coming to an end as we approached our 90-day visa, we weren't sure if taking a 2 week detour into Honduras for scuba diving certification was worth it or not, as that would cut our time in Nicaragua in half \comment{link to NI post}. In the end - with some convincing from our campervan friends - we took the plunge (pun intended) and soon our initial plan of only 4 days of scuba diving quickly turned into a full week as we fell in love with the tranquility and awe of life underwater. 

If you were to look at a map\comment{I think you should link our map!}, you would see that we made quite the trek to get to go diving in the first place. We started on the Pacific Coast of El Salvador/Honduras, drove all the way up to the Caribbean coast, and took a one hour ferry just to get to the island of Utila; an island known for being backpacker-friendly with very affordable certification options. This island is so tiny that bringing a campervan over would not have been feasible as the roads are not big enough to drive on. Legend has it that the one time that a campervan did appear on the island, the mayor and the chief of police were divided as to whether it could stay, with the mayor inviting the campervan to personally stay on his property to settle the dispute. So for this one week, the three of us stayed at a backpacker hostel that provided accomodation to accompany the scuba certification. 

Our first day there, we were horrified and disheartened and absolutely were not sure how we were going to last the week. Here's why: (1) the hostel kitchen was absolutely disgusting, like flies in the fridge disgusting, (2) we were in a shared dorm with Arya and sleeping on the top bunks which wasn't ideal for her, (3) Arya was barking at newcomers at dusk time. I realize how old we sound saying that being in a shared dorm room was not great but, to be fair, we have been living in our own space this entire time and we had originally been told we were going to have a private room because of the dog. Luckily, the next day we were able to move into a very nice private room that had AC (which we actually didn't use) and a private bathroom. This change improved things so much. For one, we could get comfortable and spread out, and Arya had a space that was hers and that helped her to calm down so much. The kitchen never improved until 5 days later, but we managed it the best we could as the affordable vegetarian options for eating out were not very plentiful. 

We worked with Arya on our first full day there as we did our course readings and quizzes. We practiced leaving her in the room without going barking mad. We practiced being calm when other people entered the hostel. We introduced her to as many guests that were comfortable meeting her (thankfully this was most of them). She very quickly got the hang of it all. 

We even benefitted from having made friends the previous night with N (from Texas!) who had shared the original dorm with us. Not only did she help us get the lay of the land around the dorms, but she also unwittingly became our backup dog sitter! Just the next day, as we were going through safety and gear with the instructor, we look up the dock and see a very familiar looking black dog trotting and goofily looking around for her humans. Quick on her feet, N jumped up and grabbed Arya to put her back in the room so that we could continue learning. (It turned out our door only stays shut when it was locked, otherwise the wind could open it just enough for a curious dog to poke her nose through…) This was not the only time this happened, but between N and our vanlife friends \comment{add link to antigua post} who came across a day later, Arya was well taken care of and watched. She was so well-loved that one of our friends had been walking her every time we were out on the boat scuba diving. As we only found this out on the last day, Arya had been parlaying everyones worry about her being too cooped up in the room into three or more walks a day, that rascal!

So Arya was enjoying the hostel life, and we were too. 

Scuba diving itself was an entirely different story. On our first day, we practiced safety and gear use right off the beach. And the first time we went under with all of our gear on, just 2 meters under surface, we were both struggling. Vrinda believed that scuba diving was not for me and that maybe I would just learn how to free dive (which I still will). I just could not stay on the floor of the ocean and I couldn't figure out this breathing thing with gear and I kept coming up. I soon got the hang of it, and the rest of the training session went smoothly.  Clark struggled when asked to practice not having air, which is something we practice multiple times throughout the whole certification. Pretending to be out of air, asking someone to share air with you, getting the spare respirator, and purging it of water before breathing in the air was definitely a bit unnatural. Clark also got the hang of this with practice. 

Now, we were ready to go to 18m! Boy was that something. We did 4 dives over the course of two days where we practiced swimming, running into issues like googles filling up with water or not having air, descending and ascending, and trying to maintain buoyancy so we are neither sinking nor floating up. It was quite a challenge when we had to navigate between the coral reef walls without touching the coral reef with our gear. I was very excited to practice my skills in this course. 

After having completed the Open Water course, and knowing how affordable we were doing this in Utila, I convinced C to stay here for a few more days and knock out the Advanced Open Water course. This course allowed us to further define our navigation skills, go down to 30m, perfect bouyancy, explore a shipwreck, and go night diving, which is a whole other world. To achieve this and still make it to Nicaragua with enough time, we ended up doing 4 dives one day and three the next. This was insane and absolutely exhausting, but whoa. The artificial \comment{not sure this is the right word} shipwreck we visited was also a time to perfect swimming with gear on and not running into the wreck itself. We even got to see a huge snapper that had been hiding in the masthead. Now, the night dive - which was our last dive - was incredible. It's dark, so we use flashlights to observe the wildlife and our group spotted an octopus, an eel, a puffer fish, massive lobsters, a lionfish, and a humongous hermit crab (that I found!). I can see why night dives are such a you either 'love it' or 'hate it' kind of dive. 

This week has us eager for more dives, and a certain Don Hickman is already thinking about future dives we could do together.
