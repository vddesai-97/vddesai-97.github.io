<em>Pura Vida</em>. Pure Life. More than just a slogan for Costa Ricans, the phrase exemplifies how many choose to live: embracing the moment, helping others, reveling in the beauty of extraordinary nature and ordinary tasks alike. 

The cynic in me was somewhat skeptical the first couple of times we heard the phrase, particularly from tourist-facing services. My thinking was along the lines of "yea, yea, we get it. You live in paradise and are leaning too far into the idea of Costa Rica being an escape for wealthy tourists to come and 'live in the moment.' You won't fool me." While there might be a small amount of truth to this (as with many popular things, the phrase has found its way onto all kinds of kitschy souvenirs and trinkets that Costa Rican TSA has surely seen too many of), the phrase is much, much more meaningful to the people here than I could have expected.

Having just completed a whirlwind week with Tusli \add{link}, we wanted to lie low for a couple of days and figure out our next steps. We could not have found a better place than <em>La Casona de Doña Julia</em>, located in the mountains about 45 minutes above San Jose (the capital). Without any commitment from us to eat at the restaurant, the manager told us we could park anywhere along the back, use their bathroom and water spigot, and wander the grounds as we pleased, all for free. With fresh mountain air and wonderful flowers in bloom, it was not hard to agree to these terms and conditions. To show our appreciation, we ordered an appetizer that afternoon and were treated to a perfectly done vegetarian version of Costa Rican food. With such hospitality and delicious food, of course we prolonged our stay an extra day and enjoyed another meal to boot. When it came time to depart, all of the employees that we had interacted with took a minute from their work to send us off with a hearty <em>"pura vida."</em>

\addpic{food, views, greenery from Dona Julia}

After our relaxing time in the mountains, we returned to San Jose to spend a couple of days in Costa Rica's only city with more than a million people. (In fact, we learned that nearly 60% of the country's 5.2 million residents live in the greater metro area of San Jose.) We wandered through the city's gastro-centric neighborhood, Barrio Escalante, visited the university grounds, and lazed in a couple of local parks. By far the highlight, however, was the most unique walking tour either of us have ever been on. 

\addpic{if you want any of barrio escalante}

From the start, we understood that our guide, J, was brimming with pride in his country and city and could not wait to impart some of this onto us. With only one other person joing ("I like to keep my groups small so that it feels more personal."), we got very comfortable with J as he took us around the city to show several of the most famous attractions and several lesser known ones as well. He was filled with fun facts, very interested in all of our travels, and eager to show us some lesser known stores so that we could experience Costa Rica less like a tourist and instead learn to "live like a local." By far the highlight of the experience, however, was that J brought a full picnic spread for us to enjoy near the end of the tour, complete with all kinds of local fruits - some familiar and some totally new - and pastries he had just purchased from a nearby Colombian bakery. Keep in mind, J had just spent three hours hauling a massive lunchbox (along with his binder for visual aids to his stories and umbrellas for inclement weather!) just for this moment. Truly an incredible amount of effort to put in to a free tour! J's kindness was totally infectious, and even as we said goodbye (<em>"Pura Vida!"</em>), we joined our very cool tourmate - L, from Germany - for a coffee and then some beers to keep the afternoon plesantries going!

Fun fact (learned from J): "Pura vida" actually comes from an old 50's Mexican comedy, in which the protagonist greets all sorts of bad luck and misadventures with the phrase. Costa Ricans loved it so much they quickly adopted it completely to the point that, today, most will be hesistant to admit that their saying has origins of another country.
