A bucket list item achieved.

With our time in the C4 countries (Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, and Nicaragua) quickly coming to an end as we approached our 90-day visa, we weren't sure if taking a 2 week detour into Honduras for scuba diving certification was worth it or not, as that would significantly cut our time in Nicaragua in half. In the end, with some convinving from our traveling friends, we took the detour and our inital plan of only 4 days of scuba diving quickly turned into a full week. 

If you were to look at a map, we started on the Pacific Coast of Honduras, drove all the way up to the Carribean coast, and took a one hour ferry to get to the island of Utila, an island known for being backpacker friendly with very affordable certification options. This island is so tiny that bringing a campervan over would not have been feasible as the roads are not big enough to drive on. It's so tiny, that the one time that a campervan did appear on the island, the mayor and the chief of police got into it, and the mayor invited the campervan to stay on his property. So for this one week, the three of us stayed at a backpacker hostel that provided accomodation with their certification. 

Our first day there, I was horrified and disheartened and absolutely did not think we were going to make it through the week. Here's why: (1) the hostal kitchen was absoluletly disgusting, like flies in the fridge disgusting, (2) we were in a shared dorm with Arya and sleeping on the top bunks which wasn't ideal for her, (3) Arya was barking at newcomers at dusk time. I realize how old we sound saying that being in a shared dorm room was not great, but to be fair, we have been living in our own space this entire time and we had been told we were going to have a private room because of the dog, so expectations had been set. Luckily, the next day we got moved into a very nice private room (bonus: for free) that had AC (which we actually didn't use) and a private bathroom. This change improved things so much. For one, we could get comfortable and spread out, and Arya had a space that was hers and helped her to calm down so much. The kitchen never improved until 5 days later, but we managed it the best we could as eating out was not appealing as a vegetarain and while being on a budget. 

We worked with Arya our first full day there as we did our course readings and quizzes. We practiced leaving her in the room and her not go barking mad. We practiced being calm when other people entered the hostel. She very quickly got the hang of it all. 

We even benefitted from having made friends the previous night with N who we were in the shared dorm with. She was very happy to have a private room all to herself when we moved out as no one moved in until her very last night. You're welcome! This is important because the next day, when we were going through safety and gear with the instructor, but Arya was in her room, which doesn't shut unless locked and we had left it unlocked...well, the wind blew open the door, and next thing you know, Arya comes trotting out all casually looking for us. N very quickly sees this and puts Arya up again before she spots us. THis was not the only time this happened, and between N and our vanlife friends who had shown up a day later, Arya was well taken care of and watched. So well-loved was she, I found out our last day, that one of our friends had been walking her every time we were out on the boat scuba diving. We had also been walking her in our free times, but how incredibily lucky was Arya (and us) for having a friend who just did that. 

So Arya was enjoying the hostal life, and we were too. 

Scuba diving itself is a whole nother story. Our first day, we practiced safety and gear use right off the beach. And the first time we went under with all of our gear on to 2 m under surface, I was struggling. I believed that scuba diving was not for me and that maybe I will just learn how to free dive (which I still will). The reason being, I couldn't stay on the floor of the ocean and I couldn't figure out this breathing thing with gear and I kept coming up. I soon got the hang of it, and the rest of the training session went smoothly.  Clark struggled with not having air, which is something we practice multiple times throughout the whole certification. Pretending to be out of air, asking someone to share air with you, getting the spare mouth breather thing, and then cleansing it of water before breathing in the air. Clark also got the hang of this with practice. 

Now, we were ready to go to 18m! Boy was that something. We did 4 dives over the course of two days where we practiced swimming, running into issues like googles filling up or no air, descending and ascending, and trying to maintian buoyancy so we are neither sinking or floating up.
