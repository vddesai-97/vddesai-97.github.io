Our second dive took place on the western edge of Utila Bay along the Black Corral Wall, where we swam for half an hour along a solid vertical shelf of corral towering next to us, sometimes reaching as many as ten meters in height. Being novices, the sheer scale and diversity of the coral structures was impressive; it's one thing to know that an entire world exists just below the surface of the ocean but it's still something totally different to witness it \comment{add link to scuba piece}. 

However, even us scuba babies could tell that the reef was not doing great, hence the words Vrinda relayed to me after we returned to the boat. Many of the corals looked totally devoid of color. Fish would occassionally wander by, but by and large we didn't see a ton of other wildlife. (We can now say that the place does get a bit more lively at night though!) While we didn't expect National Geographic level activity, it was clear that the area had seen better days. Coral reefs are one of the most biodiverse places in our ocean (and our planet): their health is vital not only to the plethora of creatures living there but also to planetary health as a whole. But our actions may be devastating these vital and beautiful places.

Modern corals have been around since the time of the dinosaurs (and ancient corals since before life moved onto land!), and have survived multiple mass extinction events in that time (Stanley, 2001). Their resiliance speaks both to the spread and diverstiy of corals around the globe over the preceding millions of years as well as the success of the symbiosis that coral polyps have developed with zooxanthellae algae. Coral structures - often made up of thousands of individual polyps - provide safe harbor and carbon dioxide for the algae, which in turn provide nutrients for the coral via photosynthesis (Red Sea Creatures, 2026). This partnership is remarkably efficient, with as much as 90% of the coral's energy coming from the algae (NOAA, 2024). The structures we see as divers actually the calcium carbonate exoskeleton designed to protect the softer polyps (and their symbiants). Over time, the polyps reproduce asexually and grow the colony, eventually building the massive reefs we see today.

While corals as a system are remarkably resislient, each individual is actually very sensitive to environmental conditions. Factors such as water temperature, nutrients, and the presence of fish combine to create a delicate balance where reefs can thrive. As we humans have historically been largely ignorant to the impact our species has on other ecosystems, it should come as no surprise that coral reefs have been in decline for as long as we have records of them - and probably for some time before (Pandolfi, 2003). Even as far back as several centuries ago, overfishing almost certainly caused disruption to environments unfortunate enough to be located near human settlements. More recently, increases in ocean temperature and pollutants have led to even more widespread destruction of these fragile environments. 

The corals that we saw that lacked most, if not all, of their color had undergone a process known as bleaching, in which the algae that provide the corals with so many of their nutrients are expelled in response to stressful conditions (Australian Institute of Marine Science (AIMS), 2025). The most common stressor to these animals are marine heatwaves, as even deviations of a single degree (Celsius) over  average. Given that the global average now exceeds this mark, it is not a surpise that we are quickly seeing the demise of these once vast ecosystems (Zeng, 2025).

"Great Barrier Reef Suffers Worst Coral Decline on Record," read one headline from last year (BBC, 2025). If the headline generates a sense of déjà vu, just know that it's not you that is going crazy, it's the planet. Over the past thirty years, NOAA and ICRI (International Coral Reef Initiative) have recorded four separate mass bleaching events, each more expansive than the last, with the latest impacting nearly 85% of the world's corals worldwide (NOAA, 2025; Reef Resilience Network, 2025). These worldwide catastrophes are extremely new to the corals - coral core records go back hundreds of years and indicate no such events occurred before the 80's but are now occuring with some regularity (AIMS, 2025; Zinke, 2014). 

\addpic{bleached coral from internet}

In addition to their beauty, the massive reef structures built by the corals are a vital part of the marine ecosystem; despite the fact that they cover only 1% of the worlds oceans, they provide habitat to around 25% of marine life (NOAA, 2025), with many species endemic to specific reefs. Because the corals literally create the framework for these ecosystems, when they undergo stress it is not only a danger to themselves, but also to most of the living things that make their home there. Not only would the loss of these environments represent a devastating blow to oceanic biodiversity, but they would also cripple fishing and tourism industries that depend on that very wildlife to thrive.
